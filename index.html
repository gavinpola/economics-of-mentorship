The Economics of Mentorship in the Age of AI

By Gavin Pola

There's a hidden economic structure beneath every apprenticeship, associate program, and junior role. For eight hundred years, it held. AI is rewinding it.

The structure is simple: train someone, capture their productivity, profit from the leverage. Medieval guilds knew it. A goldsmith took on apprentices not from charity but because, after years of patient instruction, he would have skilled labor generating income he could not produce alone. Law firms still operate on this logic. So do consulting firms. So does every company with an entry-level hiring program.

The underlying logic has been consistent for centuries: any individual can only accomplish so much in a day. To scale beyond personal capacity, you need to bring others into the work. Those others need to be trained. Training costs time and money, but it pays back: the trainer captures a share of what the trainee produces. This is why anyone bothers to teach anyone anything in a professional context.

Citrini Research's February 2026 report, "The 2028 Global Intelligence Crisis," imagined a future where AI capabilities displaced enough white-collar workers to trigger a consumption collapse. The scenario was deliberately extreme. But it pointed to something the broader debate has mostly missed: not whether automation will displace jobs, but what happens when training people isn't a given.

That structure is now shifting.

The Bottleneck Shifts

The constraint of output used to be time in the day. A senior partner could bill 2,000 hours per year. A software architect could ship so many features. A consultant could serve so many clients (and produce so many slides). To scale beyond personal capacity required bringing others into the work, training them, supervising their output, accepting lower quality initially in exchange for greater throughput eventually.

Agentic AI systems change where the constraint sits. Output is no longer bounded by headcount in the same way. A system can be adjusted directly, iterated with in real time, deployed across tasks that previously required systematic mentorship. The fidelity to intent is higher. There is less interpretation, no onboarding curve, no risk that the trained person leaves for a competitor and takes the investment with them.

Consider what this looks like in practice. Claude Code can now handle multi-week software projects that once required months to onboard a junior engineer. The cost: roughly $200 per month in API calls. A junior software engineer in San Francisco costs $8,000 per month in salary alone—before benefits, equipment, office space, or the manager's time supervising their work. The output quality is often higher. The knowledge persists.

The leverage comes from systems now, not people. This is a different story than the familiar one about AI automating jobs. It focuses on something more specific: what happens to the economic incentive to train people when scaling no longer requires them.

The Numbers

In February 2026, Jack Dorsey cut 4,000 employees at Block—roughly 40 percent of the company. His explanation was blunt: "Intelligence tools have changed what it means to build and run a company. A significantly smaller team, using the tools we're building, can do more and do it better." He predicted most companies would follow within a year. The stock surged 17 percent on the announcement.

The pattern is visible across sectors. Recent college graduate unemployment hit 4.8 percent in mid-2025, according to the Federal Reserve Bank of New York—exceeding the national average for the first time in recorded history. Entry-level hiring has dropped 17 percent since 2019. Job postings for new graduates fell 15 percent year over year; applications per role jumped 30 percent. In the UK, 1.2 million applications were submitted for 17,000 graduate roles.

In professional services, PwC announced in September 2025 that 200 fewer graduates would join its UK offices, with US associate ranks projected to decline by a third by 2028. According to Artificial Lawyer's 2025 survey, 86 percent of large law firms plan to grow their associate ranks—but only 35 percent plan to grow their first-year classes. The pyramid is inverting.

The short-term arithmetic is sound. Whether it accounts for what we might lose downstream is another question.

What Follows

The immediate economics are clear. The downstream consequences are not. When the economic incentive to train people weakens, several things follow.

The first is a question of knowledge transfer. Mentorship has never been purely transactional. It transfers tacit knowledge: how to read a room, when to push back on a client, the difference between what is technically correct and what is actually wise. This knowledge cannot be written in manuals or encoded in training data. It moves through proximity, observation, relationship. It is unclear how this transfer happens when the economic reason for the relationship diminishes.

The second is a pipeline question. If we hire fewer juniors in 2025, who becomes the senior talent in 2030? Judgment cannot be fine-tuned into AI systems if it was never developed in people. The short-term math may look sound while quietly breaking something downstream.

The third is a question of access. Entry-level jobs have served as an on-ramp for people without existing networks or inherited capital. Mentors provide more than instruction; they validate credentials, make introductions, vouch for capability—assets that a resume alone cannot convey. The traditional pathways into professional careers, imperfect as they were, created routes for wealth-building. As those pathways narrow, the question becomes who finds alternatives and who does not.

Not every AI bet pays off. Klarna replaced 700 customer service workers with AI, then reversed course after quality collapsed. The CEO told Fast Company: "We focused too much on efficiency. The result was lower quality." Wharton's Ethan Mollick questioned whether any company could achieve 50 percent efficiency gains this quickly. Some of what's called AI transformation may be pandemic overhiring finally correcting—with AI as a convenient narrative.

The Question

For eight hundred years, training people was good business. The leverage model worked. Guilds became industries. Firms became institutions. Knowledge passed between generations through economic relationships that benefited both sides.

The math is different now. What this means for companies, for workers, for how expertise reproduces across time, remains to be seen. It may be that new structures emerge to replace the old ones. It may be that the shift is slower than early data suggests. It may be that the effects concentrate in specific sectors while others remain untouched.

What seems clear is that an economic logic that held for centuries is no longer holding in the same way. The question is not whether this is happening. The data suggests it already is. The question is what comes next—and whether we will notice the absence of what we failed to build before the consequences become irreversible.
